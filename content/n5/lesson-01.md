### Word-by-word details

**1. 私（わたし）— 我**
- 例文: 私は学生です。
- 翻譯: 我是學生。
- Common mistake: Learners often drop は after 私, saying ❌ 私学生です。は is required to mark the topic.
- Memory tip: 私 looks complex but appears constantly — think of the ㄙ-shaped radical on the left as "私人的（private）"，禾+ム, and just memorize the whole shape as "me."

**2. あなた — 你**
- 例文: あなたは先生ですか。
- 翻譯: 你是老師嗎？
- Common mistake: Unlike Chinese 你, あなた sounds distant/awkward when used with someone you know (e.g., a boss or elder) — Japanese usually prefers using the person's name + さん instead of あなた.
- Memory tip: Overuse this word only with strangers or in textbooks; in real conversation, favor 名前＋さん.

**3. 学生（がくせい）— 學生**
- 例文: 私は学生です。
- 翻譯: 我是學生。
- Common mistake: Confusing 学生 (student, any level) with 先生 (teacher) because the sentence shape is identical — 私は学生／先生です differ only by one word, so learners misread quickly.
- Memory tip: 学 (study) + 生 (life/person) = "a person who studies." Both kanji look almost the same as Chinese 學生.

**4. 先生（せんせい）— 老師**
- 例文: 田中さんは先生です。
- 翻譯: 田中先生是老師。
- Common mistake: Chinese speakers often call themselves 先生 out of politeness copied from Chinese ("我是陳先生"), but in Japanese 先生 is a respectful title for others (teachers, doctors) — never used to refer to yourself.
- Memory tip: 先 (before/ahead) + 生 (life) = "one born before you," i.e., someone more experienced — a teacher.

**5. 会社員（かいしゃいん）— 公司職員**
- 例文: 兄は会社員です。
- 翻譯: 哥哥是公司職員。
- Common mistake: Learners try to say "商人" or invent 商人員; 会社員 is the standard word for a generic office worker, regardless of company type.
- Memory tip: 会社 (company) + 員 (member) = "a member of a company."

**6. 日本（にほん）— 日本**
- 例文: 私は日本人ではありません。
- 翻譯: 我不是日本人。
- Common mistake: Mispronouncing as にっぽん is not wrong (both readings exist), but learners sometimes read 日 with Chinese-influenced intuition as じつ instead of に in this compound.
- Memory tip: 日 (sun) + 本 (origin) = "origin of the sun" — Land of the Rising Sun.

**7. 台湾（たいわん）— 台灣**
- 例文: 私は台湾人です。
- 翻譯: 我是台灣人。
- Common mistake: None phonetically — the reading たいわん is very close to Mandarin, but learners sometimes forget to add 人 to say the nationality (❌ 私は台湾です meaning "I am Taiwan").
- Memory tip: Almost identical sound to Mandarin "Táiwān" — one of the easiest words in the whole course.

**8. 中国（ちゅうごく）— 中國**
- 例文: 彼は中国人です。
- 翻譯: 他是中國人。
- Common mistake: Reading 中 as ちゅう is correct here, but learners later confuse it with 中（なか, "inside") from Lesson 7 — same kanji, different reading depending on the word.
- Memory tip: 中 (middle) + 国 (country) = "the middle kingdom," a direct calque of 中國.

**9. 人（ひと／じん）— 人**
- 例文: あの人は先生です。
- 翻譯: 那個人是老師。
- Common mistake: Learners mix up the two readings — ひと for "a person" standing alone (あの人) vs. じん attached after a country name for nationality (日本人). Using ひと after a country name (❌ 日本ひと) is wrong.
- Memory tip: Same kanji as Chinese 人; alone = ひと, glued to a place name = じん.

**10. 名前（なまえ）— 名字、姓名**
- 例文: お名前は何ですか。
- 翻譯: 您的名字是什麼？
- Common mistake: Learners answer 名前 questions with only their given name when a full name is expected, or vice versa — 名前 covers either, so context decides.
- Memory tip: 名 (name) + 前 (front) = "what comes before/is presented as your name."

**11. です — 是**
- 例文: これは本です。
- 翻譯: 這是書。
- Common mistake: Treating です as a full verb that can be negated like a regular verb (❌ ですない) instead of using the correct negative ではありません.
- Memory tip: です glues to the end of nouns/adjectives to make a sentence polite — think of it as a polite full stop.

**12. はい — 是、好的**
- 例文: はい、そうです。
- 翻譯: 是，沒錯。
- Common mistake: Using はい alone to mean "here you go" (handing something) — that is a different usage; in answers はい strictly means "yes/correct."
- Memory tip: はい sounds like a short, crisp "yeah!" — easy to remember by sound alone.

**13. いいえ — 不是**
- 例文: いいえ、学生じゃありません。
- 翻譯: 不，我不是學生。
- Common mistake: Learners think いいえ is rude like a blunt "no" — in fact Japanese often softens it further with いえ、いや in casual speech, but いいえ itself is already polite and fine in formal contexts.
- Memory tip: Two い sounds like a double head-shake — "no, no."

**14. よろしく — 請多指教**
- 例文: よろしくお願いします。
- 翻譯: 請多多指教。
- Common mistake: Directly translating "please" from Chinese 請 and using よろしく alone in situations where お願いします should follow — よろしく by itself sounds incomplete/casual.
- Memory tip: Always pair with お願いします in polite contexts; think of them as one unit at this stage.

**15. 初めまして（はじめまして）— 初次見面**
- 例文: 初めまして、田中です。
- 翻譯: 初次見面，我是田中。
- Common mistake: Using 初めまして every time you greet someone you've already met — it is strictly a "nice to meet you for the first time" phrase, not a general greeting like こんにちは.
- Memory tip: 初め (beginning) + まして (polite verb ending) = "for the first time [we meet]."

**16. 何（なに／なん）— 什麼**
- 例文: お名前は何ですか。
- 翻譯: 您的名字是什麼？
- Common mistake: Choosing the wrong reading — なん before です/だ and before counters (何ですか、何歳), but なに in most other cases (何を食べますか). Beginners often default to one reading everywhere.
- Memory tip: Say it out loud in context — "nan-desu-ka" flows more easily than "nani-desu-ka," which is a good rule of thumb.

**17. ～さん — 先生／小姐（敬稱）**
- 例文: 田中さんは会社員です。
- 翻譯: 田中先生是公司職員。
- Common mistake: Attaching さん to your own name (❌ 私はマービンさんです) — さん is only used for others, never for yourself.
- Memory tip: さん is a universal, gender-neutral politeness suffix — like "Mr./Ms." combined into one.

**18. ～人（～じん）— ……人**
- 例文: 彼女は韓国人です。
- 翻譯: 她是韓國人。
- Common mistake: Attaching じん after words that aren't countries, or forgetting to attach it at all (see #7's note); also confusing with 人 (ひと) used standalone.
- Memory tip: Country name + 人 = nationality, a very regular, reliable pattern in Japanese.

**19. 友達（ともだち）— 朋友**
- 例文: 山田さんは私の友達です。
- 翻譯: 山田先生是我的朋友。
- Common mistake: Learners add さん after 友達 when talking about their own friend to a third person (❌ 私の友達さん) — さん is not attached to 友達 itself.
- Memory tip: 友 (friend) + 達 (plural/group marker) = "friend(s)."

**20. 大学（だいがく）— 大學**
- 例文: 私は大学の学生です。
- 翻譯: 我是大學生。
- Common mistake: Saying 大学生の私 or word-order mistakes copied from Chinese; also confusing 大学 (university) with 大学校 which doesn't exist as a standard word.
- Memory tip: 大 (big) + 学 (study) = "a big place of study" — university.

## ② Kanji (5): 人, 日, 本, 学, 生

**人 — Meaning: 人 (person)**
- On-reading: ジン、ニン
- Kun-reading: ひと
- Words: 日本人, 台湾人, 一人 (ひとり)
- Example sentence: 私は台湾人です。
- Stroke order: 2 strokes — a left-leaning stroke first (from top down-left), then a right-leaning stroke crossing it near the top, like a person standing with two legs.
- Chinese difference: Identical shape to Chinese 人, and the meaning is unchanged — one of the easiest kanji for Chinese speakers. Just remember it also has a native ひと reading, unlike Chinese where 人 has only one reading.

**日 — Meaning: 日 (sun, day, Japan)**
- On-reading: ニチ、ジツ
- Kun-reading: ひ、か
- Words: 日本, 何日 (preview), 日曜日 (preview)
- Example sentence: 今日は何曜日ですか。（used loosely here as a preview; core use this lesson: 日本）
- Stroke order: 4 strokes — outer rectangle drawn in three strokes (left side, top+right side, bottom), then the middle horizontal line last.
- Chinese difference: Same shape as Chinese 日, but Chinese only reads it as "rì"; Japanese has multiple readings (にち/じつ/ひ/か) depending on the word — a key adjustment for Chinese speakers used to one reading per character.

**本 — Meaning: 本 (book, origin, main)**
- On-reading: ホン
- Kun-reading: もと
- Words: 日本, 本 (ほん, "book" — Lesson 14 vocab)
- Example sentence: これは本です。
- Stroke order: 5 strokes — write 木 (tree) first, then add the extra horizontal stroke through the base to mark "the root/origin."
- Chinese difference: Same character as Chinese 本, meaning largely overlaps ("book," "origin"), but in Japanese 本 as a standalone word almost always means "book," while in Chinese 本 alone rarely means "book" (本 is more often a measure word or "origin/this").

**学 — Meaning: 學 (study, learning)**
- On-reading: ガク
- Kun-reading: まな.ぶ
- Words: 学生, 大学, 学校 (preview)
- Example sentence: 私は学生です。
- Stroke order: 8 strokes — top element (a small crown-like shape) first, then the covering line, then 子 at the bottom.
- Chinese difference: This is the simplified-style Japanese form of 學; it looks different from Traditional Chinese 學 (used in Taiwan) but is much closer to Simplified Chinese 学. Taiwanese learners should note Japanese kanji forms sometimes match Simplified Chinese rather than Traditional Chinese.

**生 — Meaning: 生 (life, birth, raw)**
- On-reading: セイ、ショウ
- Kun-reading: い.きる、う.まれる、なま
- Words: 学生, 先生, 誕生日 (preview)
- Example sentence: 田中さんは先生です。
- Stroke order: 5 strokes — top short horizontal stroke, then a vertical stroke down through the middle, crossed by two more horizontal strokes, finishing with the long bottom horizontal stroke.
- Chinese difference: Identical shape and core meaning to Chinese 生, but Japanese uses far more readings for this single character (せい/しょう/い.きる/う.まれる/なま) depending on context, versus a single Mandarin reading "shēng."

## ③ Grammar (2 points)

### Grammar 1 — 〜は〜です ("A is B")
**Meaning:** States that A (the topic) is B.
**Structure:** A + は + B + です
**Examples:**
1. 私は学生です。(I am a student.)
2. 私は台湾人です。(I am Taiwanese.)
3. マービンさんは先生です。(Marvin is a teacher.)

**Common mistakes (Chinese speakers):**
- ❌ 私学生です → ⭕ 私は学生です — the topic marker は is almost always required and cannot be dropped the way Chinese drops "是" markers in casual speech.
- ❌ 私は学生だ です (mixing plain and polite forms) → ⭕ 私は学生です.

**Exercises (translate into Japanese):**
1. I am a student.
2. I am Taiwanese.
3. She is a teacher. (使用 彼女)
4. Mr. Tanaka is an office worker.

### Grammar 2 — 〜ですか (question form)
**Meaning:** Turns a です-sentence into a yes/no question by adding か, no word order change needed.
**Structure:** A + は + B + ですか
**Examples:**
1. 学生ですか。→ はい、学生です。(Are you a student? → Yes, I am.)
2. 先生ですか。→ いいえ、先生ではありません。(Are you a teacher? → No, I am not.)
3. あなたは台湾人ですか。→ はい、台湾人です。

**Common mistakes (Chinese speakers):**
- ❌ Adding a question mark but forgetting か (writing あなたは学生です？) — Japanese needs the spoken/written particle か, not just intonation, in standard written form.
- ❌ 学生ですか？はい、そうです学生 → ⭕ はい、学生です — don't tack the noun onto そうです redundantly.

**Exercises (translate into Japanese):**
1. Are you a teacher?
2. Is he Japanese? (使用 彼)
3. Are you a student? → No, I am not a student.

## 🇹🇼 Chinese Notes

- **先生（せんせい）**: In Chinese, 先生 is often used as a polite title for any adult man (similar to "Mr."). In Japanese, 先生 specifically means "teacher" or a professional such as a doctor — it is never used to mean "Mr." for an ordinary person, and you should never call yourself 先生.
- **人（ひと／じん）**: The character is identical to Chinese 人, but Japanese splits its use into two readings depending on position: ひと when standing alone referring to "a person," and じん when attached after a country/place name to mean nationality. Chinese uses 人 the same way in both cases without a reading change.
- **学（学生, 大学）**: The Japanese kanji 学 visually matches Simplified Chinese 学, not Traditional Chinese 學 used in Taiwan. Don't be surprised when Japanese kanji forms sometimes look "simplified" compared to what you learned in school — this is a general pattern to watch for throughout the course, not a special exception.
