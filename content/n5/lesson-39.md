### Word-by-word details

**1. すみません — 不好意思、抱歉**
- 例文: すみません、駅はどこですか。
- 翻譯: 不好意思，請問車站在哪裡？
- Common mistake: Learning only the "sorry" meaning and missing that すみません also means "excuse me" (to get attention) and even a light "thank you for the trouble" — one word covers three jobs that Chinese splits into 對不起／不好意思／謝謝.
- Memory tip: Think of すみません as an all-purpose "polite interruption word" — apology, attention-grabber, and soft thanks in one.

**2. ごめんなさい — 對不起**
- 例文: 遅れてごめんなさい。
- 翻譯: 對不起，我遲到了。
- Common mistake: Using ごめんなさい in formal/business settings — it's personal and casual, fine with friends and family, but すみません or 申し訳ありません is safer at work.
- Memory tip: ごめん alone is very casual (close friends); adding なさい makes it a notch more polite, but it's still an everyday, casual apology.

**3. ありがとう — 謝謝**
- 例文: 手伝ってくれて、ありがとう。
- 翻譯: 謝謝你幫我。
- Common mistake: Using bare ありがとう in formal contexts without ございます — ありがとうございます is the polite/formal version; ありがとう alone is casual, for friends and family.
- Memory tip: Add ございます to dress it up for politeness, the same way です dresses up casual speech.

**4. どういたしまして — 不客氣**
- 例文: 「ありがとう。」「どういたしまして。」
- 翻譯: 「謝謝。」「不客氣。」
- Common mistake: Overusing it the way English overuses "you're welcome" — natural spoken Japanese often prefers いえいえ, いいえ, or no verbal reply at all in casual settings; どういたしまして can sound a bit formal/textbook-ish among friends.
- Memory tip: Always safe and correct in polite/formal contexts — a reliable default answer to ありがとうございます.

**5. お願いします — 拜託、麻煩你**
- 例文: これ、お願いします。
- 翻譯: 麻煩你，這個給我。
- Common mistake: Treating it as identical to ください — お願いします is broader (requests actions, favors, services, or handing something over for processing), while ください attaches right after a noun or a verb's て form for a more literal "please give/do."
- Memory tip: お願いします is the safe, all-purpose "please" — usable from ordering food to asking a big favor.

**6. 失礼します — 不好意思、告辭**
- 例文: 失礼します。（entering a teacher's office）
- 翻譯: 不好意思，打擾了（進門時說）。
- Common mistake: Mixing up when to use 失礼します (announcing your own small intrusion — entering, stepping out mid-meeting, hanging up the phone) versus すみません (getting attention/apologizing generally) — they overlap but 失礼します centers on your own action.
- Memory tip: Literally "I will be rude" — said right before doing something that could be seen as slightly impolite, like entering someone's office.

**7. お大事に — 請保重（對病人）**
- 例文: お大事に。早く元気になってください。
- 翻譯: 請保重，希望你早日康復。
- Common mistake: Using お大事に as a general farewell to someone who isn't sick — this phrase is reserved specifically for wishing a sick or injured person well.
- Memory tip: Shares 大事 with 大丈夫 (Lesson 23) — お大事に = "please treasure/take care of [your health]."

**8. お疲れ様（でした）— 辛苦了**
- 例文: お疲れ様でした。今日もありがとうございました。
- 翻譯: 辛苦了，今天也謝謝你。
- Common mistake: Trying to translate this literally instead of learning it as a fixed social ritual — Chinese has no single routine phrase for "thanks for the effort you just put in," said at the end of nearly any shared work or class, even to people who did less than you.
- Memory tip: Said constantly in Japanese workplaces and schools whenever shared effort wraps up — it's a polite ritual, not a literal judgment of how hard someone worked.

**9. 頑張って — 加油**
- 例文: 明日、試験ですね。頑張ってください。
- 翻譯: 明天要考試對吧，加油！
- Common mistake: Using 頑張って the way English uses "good luck" (implying chance/fate) — 頑張って is about effort and perseverance, encouraging someone to try hard, not wishing them luck.
- Memory tip: From 頑張る (Lesson 16, "to do one's best") — 頑張って is its casual て-form command/encouragement.

**10. おめでとう（ございます）— 恭喜**
- 例文: 合格、おめでとうございます！
- 翻譯: 恭喜你考上了！
- Common mistake: Using おめでとう for events that aren't a clear, already-achieved good outcome — it celebrates good news that has *happened* (passing an exam, a birthday, a marriage), unlike 頑張って, which encourages something *about to happen*.
- Memory tip: おめでとう celebrates the *after*; 頑張って encourages the *before*.

**11. いってきます — 我出門了**
- 例文: 「いってきます。」「いってらっしゃい。」
- 翻譯: 「我出門了。」「路上小心，慢走！」
- Common mistake: Translating it too literally and using it when leaving a friend's house — this set phrase is specifically for leaving your own home base (or workplace, temporarily) with an implied return.
- Memory tip: 行って (go) + 来ます (come, Lesson 6) = "I'm going and (will be) coming back."

**12. いってらっしゃい — 路上小心（歡送語）**
- 例文: いってらっしゃい。気をつけてね。
- 翻譯: 慢走，路上小心喔。
- Common mistake: Using this to see off someone leaving for good (not returning) — いってらっしゃい specifically responds to いってきます, implying the person is expected back.
- Memory tip: Always said as a call-and-response pair with いってきます — memorize them together.

**13. ただいま — 我回來了**
- 例文: ただいま。ただいま帰りました。
- 翻譯: 我回來了。
- Common mistake: Confusing greeting ただいま (I'm home) with 今 (いま, "now") even though they share 今 — as a greeting, ただいま is a fixed idiom, not a literal statement about time.
- Memory tip: Pairs as a call-and-response with おかえりなさい, just like いってきます pairs with いってらっしゃい.

**14. おかえりなさい — 你回來啦**
- 例文: おかえりなさい。晩ご飯できていますよ。
- 翻譯: 你回來啦，晚飯做好囉。
- Common mistake: Shortening it in a way that loses the greeting meaning — casual friends may say just おかえり, but it's still this fixed idiom, not a literal statement built freely from 帰る.
- Memory tip: 帰る (Lesson 6, to return home) + なさい (polite imperative ending) = a fixed idiom welcoming someone back.

**15. お邪魔します — 打擾了（進門時）**
- 例文: お邪魔します。
- 翻譯: 打擾了（進入別人家時說）。
- Common mistake: Using this when entering your *own* home (that's ただいま) — お邪魔します is specifically for entering *someone else's* space as a guest.
- Memory tip: 邪魔 (じゃま, nuisance/obstacle) — literally "I will be a nuisance [by entering]," a humble acknowledgment of imposing.

**16. 気をつけて（ください）— 小心、注意安全**
- 例文: 道が濡れていますから、気をつけてください。
- 翻譯: 路上很濕，請小心。
- Common mistake: Treating 危ない (Lesson 34, "dangerous!", an exclamation for immediate danger) as interchangeable with 気をつけて (a general caution or farewell wish) — they serve different moments.
- Memory tip: 気 (spirit/attention, from 天気, Lesson 13) + つける (to attach) = "attach your attention [to safety]."

**17. お先に失礼します — 我先告辭了**
- 例文: お先に失礼します。お疲れ様でした。
- 翻譯: 我先告辭了，辛苦了。
- Common mistake: Just saying さようなら when leaving work before others — it sounds oddly final/distant for a workplace; お先に失礼します is the expected, humble way to announce leaving before colleagues.
- Memory tip: お先に (ahead of others) + 失礼します (excuse my rudeness) = "pardon me for leaving ahead of you."

**18. よくできました — 做得很好**
- 例文: よくできました！次も頑張りましょう。
- 翻譯: 做得很好！下次也繼續加油。
- Common mistake: Using よくできました toward someone of equal or higher status — this phrase has a teacher-to-student, evaluative feel (like grading), so saying it to a boss or elder can sound condescending.
- Memory tip: よく (well) + できました (was able to do, past of できる, Lesson 32) = "you were able to do it well" — classic teacher's praise.

**19. お陰様で — 託您的福**
- 例文: 「お元気ですか。」「はい、お陰様で元気です。」
- 翻譯: 「你好嗎？」「託您的福，我很好。」
- Common mistake: Taking it too literally as thanking the specific listener present — it's often a general, humble expression of gratitude for one's own good fortune/health, not necessarily crediting that one listener directly.
- Memory tip: 陰 (shade/shadow, "in the shadow of your support") + 様 (polite suffix) = "thanks to your [unseen] support."

**20. お世話になります — 承蒙照顧（開場白）**
- 例文: これから、お世話になります。よろしくお願いします。
- 翻譯: 從今以後要請您多多照顧了，請多指教。
- Common mistake: Assuming this only fits *after* receiving help — it's also used proactively, *before* receiving any help, as a polite opening when starting something new (a new job, class, or neighborhood).
- Memory tip: 世話 (せわ, care/looking after) — "I will be under your care," a humble social phrase with no exact single Chinese equivalent.

**21. ようこそ — 歡迎**
- 例文: ようこそ、日本へ！
- 翻譯: 歡迎來到日本！
- Common mistake: Using ようこそ as a casual daily "welcome" said to a colleague arriving at the office — it's reserved for genuinely welcoming someone to a place or event (a country, a party, a welcome ceremony), not routine arrivals.
- Memory tip: Often seen on banners at events: ようこそ〇〇へ — "Welcome to ___."

**22. お邪魔しました — 打擾了（離開時）**
- 例文: 今日はありがとうございました。お邪魔しました。
- 翻譯: 今天謝謝招待，打擾了。
- Common mistake: Forgetting to switch tense — お邪魔します is said upon *entering*, お邪魔しました (past) upon *leaving* someone else's home; using the wrong one at the wrong moment is a common slip.
- Memory tip: Same present/past pair-logic as いってきます／ただいま — the tense marks which end of the visit you're at.

**23. お元気で — 請保重、多保重**
- 例文: それでは、お元気で。
- 翻譯: 那麼，請多保重。
- Common mistake: Confusing お元気で (a farewell wish for long-term well-being, often before a long separation) with 気をつけて (caution for one specific short trip) — お元気で is the bigger, longer-range farewell.
- Memory tip: 元気 (healthy/energetic, Lesson 12) + で (particle) = "stay in a healthy state."

**24. 失礼しました — 剛才失禮了**
- 例文: 失礼しました。もう一度お願いします。
- 翻譯: 剛才不好意思，請再說一次。
- Common mistake: Confusing present 失礼します (about to do something slightly intrusive) with past 失礼しました (apologizing for something that already happened) — tense marks whether the "rudeness" is upcoming or already occurred.
- Memory tip: Same present/past logic as お邪魔します／お邪魔しました.

**25. どうぞよろしく（お願いします）— 請多指教（回應）**
- 例文: 「初めまして、田中です。」「どうぞよろしく。」
- 翻譯: 「初次見面，我是田中。」「請多指教。」
- Common mistake: Assuming どうぞよろしく can only ever be a reply — it can be said first too, though it's most often heard as a response to someone else's よろしくお願いします or 初めまして.
- Memory tip: Recall よろしく from Lesson 1 — どうぞ (please/go ahead) simply adds extra politeness in front of it.

## ② Kanji (1): 元

**元 — Meaning: 元 (origin, base)**
- On-reading: ゲン、ガン
- Kun-reading: もと
- Words: 元気 (げんき, Lesson 12), お元気で (this lesson)
- Example sentence: それでは、お元気で。
- Stroke order: 4 strokes — write the top two short horizontal strokes first, then the long sweeping diagonal stroke down to the left, finishing with the vertical hook on the right (like 二 sitting on top of a pair of "legs").
- Chinese difference: Same character and core meaning as Chinese 元 ("origin, base"). One key difference: in Chinese, 元/圆 is also the word for currency ("yuan"), but Japan's currency kanji is 円 (Lesson 2), not 元 — don't assume 元 means "money/dollar" in a Japanese sentence the way it might in Chinese.

## ③ Grammar (3 points)

### Grammar 1 — 使役形〜させます (causative form)
**Meaning:** Expresses making or letting someone do something.
**Structure:** る-verbs: drop る, add させる；う-verbs: change the final u-sound to a-sound + せる (話す→話させる)；irregular: する→させる, 来る→来させる
**Examples:**
1. 先生は学生に本を読ませます。(The teacher makes/has the students read a book.)
2. 母は子供に野菜を食べさせます。(The mother makes her child eat vegetables.)
3. 部長は私に出張させました。(The manager had me go on a business trip.)

**Common mistakes (Chinese speakers):**
- Confusing the causative (させます, "make/let someone do") with the passive (られます, Lesson 38, "have something done to you") — the two conjugations look similar for some verb groups and are easy to mix up conceptually; causative means the causer makes someone else act, passive means the subject is on the receiving end of an action.
- Forgetting the particle can shift meaning: に generally marks a person who has some agency in the action, while を is used especially with intransitive verbs (子供を歩かせる, "make the child walk").

**Exercises (translate into Japanese):**
1. The teacher made the students write kanji.
2. My parents let me study abroad.
3. The manager made him wait.

### Grammar 2 — 〜てもらいたいです／〜てほしいです (want someone to do)
**Meaning:** Expresses a wish for someone *else* to do something — not for yourself.
**Structure:** Verb-て form + もらいたいです or + ほしいです
**Examples:**
1. 早く返事をしてほしいです。(I want you to reply soon.)
2. 手伝ってもらいたいです。(I'd like you to help me.)
3. 子供に元気に育ってほしいです。(I want my child to grow up healthy.)

**Common mistakes (Chinese speakers):**
- Using 〜たいです (Lesson 19, wanting to do something yourself) when the wish is actually about someone *else's* action — saying ❌手伝いたいです when you mean "I want you to help me" actually says "I want to help [someone]." The correct form is 手伝ってほしいです.
- Forgetting that てほしい attaches to *another person's* action, never the speaker's own.

**Exercises (translate into Japanese):**
1. I want you to come to the party.
2. I want him to be careful.
3. I want my child to be happy.

### Grammar 3 — 〜させてください (please let me do)
**Meaning:** Politely asks permission to do something oneself.
**Structure:** Causative-て form + ください
**Examples:**
1. 少し休ませてください。(Please let me rest a little.)
2. この仕事をやらせてください。(Please let me do this job.)
3. 説明させてください。(Please let me explain.)

**Common mistakes (Chinese speakers):**
- Confusing させてください (asking permission for the *speaker* to act) with してください (Lesson 22, asking the *listener* to act) — the meaning of "who does the action" flips between the two.
- Over-using させてください for minor requests where a simple 〜てもいいですか (Lesson 23) would do — させてください implies a stronger personal wish/eagerness to do something, not just casual permission-seeking.

**Exercises (translate into Japanese):**
1. Please let me try.
2. Please let me pay.
3. Please let me think about it a little.

## ④ Reading

今日は日本語学校の最後の日でした。先生に「今まで本当にお世話になりました」と言いました。先生は「よく頑張りましたね。よくできました」と言ってくれました。クラスメートとも「お疲れ様でした」と挨拶しました。友達は「これからも日本語の勉強を続けてほしい」と言いました。私は「頑張ります」と答えました。学校を出るとき、「失礼します」と言って、みんなに「お元気で」と伝えました。先生は「気をつけて帰ってくださいね」と言いました。とても懐かしい一年でした。

**Comprehension questions:**
1. 「私」は先生に何と言いましたか。
2. 友達は「私」に何をしてほしいと言いましたか。
3. 先生は「私」が帰るとき、何と言いましたか。

**Answer key:**
1. 「今まで本当にお世話になりました」と言いました。
2. これからも日本語の勉強を続けてほしいと言いました。
3. 「気をつけて帰ってくださいね」と言いました。

## ⑤ Listening

**Answer key:**

## 🇹🇼 Chinese Notes

- **A phrase for every social moment**: Japanese has many more fixed, situation-specific set phrases than Chinese for the same everyday moments — leaving home (いってきます), returning (ただいま), entering someone's house (お邪魔します), finishing work (お疲れ様でした), or parting for a long time (お元気で) each get their own dedicated expression. Chinese speakers often reach for one flexible phrase (再見, 謝謝, 辛苦了) to cover several of these moments, but in Japanese using the wrong one, or skipping it, can feel noticeably off — these phrases function almost like required social ritual, not optional politeness.
- **お疲れ様 has no single Chinese equivalent**: 辛苦了 is the closest translation, but お疲れ様(でした) is used far more routinely in Japan — said at the end of nearly any shared task, meeting, or class, regardless of how much effort each person actually put in. Treat it as an automatic ritual phrase, not a literal judgment of effort.
- **元 is not currency in Japanese**: Unlike Chinese, where 元/圆 doubles as the word for money ("yuan"), Japanese keeps 元 strictly to its "origin/base" meaning; the currency kanji is 円 (Lesson 2). Don't let the shared character mislead you into reading 元 as "money" in a Japanese sentence.
